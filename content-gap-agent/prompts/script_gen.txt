## STRUCTURAL FRAMEWORK: MINTO PYRAMID PRINCIPLE

Every script MUST follow the Minto Pyramid Principle — lead with the conclusion, then support it with grouped arguments, then layer in proof. This means:

1. **Lead with the answer (HOOK + SCENE 1):** State the single most important conclusion or claim upfront. The viewer should know your core message within the first 15 seconds. Do not "build up" to it — deliver it immediately.
2. **Group supporting arguments logically (SCENE 2 + SCENE 3):** Present 2 supporting ideas that directly defend the hook's claim. Each argument should be mutually exclusive (no overlap) and collectively exhaustive (cover the full case). Order them by impact: strongest argument first.
3. **Ground with evidence (SCENE 4):** Every argument needs proof. Use one specific, concrete data point or case study. No unsupported assertions.
4. **Synthesize and elevate (SCENE 5 + CTA):** Tie the pyramid together by zooming out to the "so what," then close with a single action.

**Minto checklist — verify before finalizing:**
- Can you state the entire video's argument in one sentence? If not, the pyramid is unclear.
- Does each scene answer "why?" or "how?" for the scene before it? If not, reorder.
- Are supporting points grouped, not listed randomly? If not, restructure.
- Is proof specific (numbers, names, timeframes)? If not, make it concrete.

## SECTION BREAKDOWN

### HOOK (3-5 seconds)
- **Pyramid role: State the governing conclusion.**
- The scroll-stopper. First words must grab attention in under 3 seconds.
- Use a bold stat, shocking question, or counterintuitive claim that IS the video's core thesis.
- Must be directly tied to the topic — no generic openers.
- Example: "Your front desk is losing 30 patients a week — and you don't know it."

### SCENE 1 — PROBLEM (8-10 seconds)
- **Pyramid role: Make the conclusion urgent by naming the pain.**
- Establish the specific pain point the viewer faces right now.
- Make it visceral and concrete. Name the frustration, not the category.
- This scene should make the viewer feel the cost of inaction — it directly supports WHY the hook's claim matters.
- Example: "After-hours calls go to voicemail. Patients give up. They call your competitor."

### SCENE 2 — INSIGHT / KEY ARGUMENT 1 (8-10 seconds)
- **Pyramid role: First supporting argument — the reframe.**
- Deliver the core revelation. One big idea that reframes the problem.
- Should create a "huh, I never thought of it that way" moment.
- This is the STRONGEST reason the viewer should care. Place it here because Minto says: strongest argument first.
- Example: "Voice AI doesn't replace your staff — it handles the 62% of calls that never needed a human."

### SCENE 3 — HOW IT WORKS / KEY ARGUMENT 2 (8-10 seconds)
- **Pyramid role: Second supporting argument — the mechanism.**
- Explain how the solution works in plain language. No jargon.
- A 3-step process or before/after contrast works well.
- This answers "okay, but how?" — the logical next question after the insight.
- Example: "Patient calls in. AI triages urgency. Books or escalates in under 45 seconds."

### SCENE 4 — PROOF / EVIDENCE (8-10 seconds)
- **Pyramid role: Concrete evidence grounding the arguments above.**
- Make it real with a specific number, case study, or vivid use case.
- Must directly validate the claims made in Scene 2 and Scene 3.
- Specificity beats generality every time. Include timeframes, percentages, outcomes.
- Example: "One clinic cut no-shows by 40% in 90 days. No extra headcount. Just smarter routing."

### SCENE 5 — BIGGER PICTURE / SYNTHESIS (5-8 seconds)
- **Pyramid role: Synthesize the pyramid — tie the conclusion, arguments, and proof into a forward-looking statement.**
- Zoom out to the "so what." Why does this matter beyond the immediate benefit?
- Connect to a trend, aspiration, or future state.
- Example: "The clinics winning in 2026 are the ones that never make a patient wait — for anything."

### CTA (5-8 seconds)
- **Pyramid role: Convert conviction into action.**
- One clear action only. Do not stack multiple CTAs.
- Be specific: "Link in bio to book a free 15-min demo" beats "check us out."
- Optional: tease the next piece of content to drive follows.

## ENCODING AND CHARACTER RULES — CRITICAL

**Use ONLY plain ASCII characters (code points 32-126).** This is mandatory to prevent encoding corruption in downstream systems.

- **Arrows:** Write "to" or "-->" or "->" instead of any Unicode arrow symbols.
- **Dashes:** Use "--" (double hyphen) for em-dashes. Do not use Unicode em-dash or en-dash characters.
- **Quotes:** Use standard straight quotes (" and ') only. Do not use curly/smart quotes.
- **Bullets:** Use "-" or "*" for list items. Do not use Unicode bullet characters.
- **Ellipsis:** Write "..." (three periods). Do not use the Unicode ellipsis character.
- **Other symbols:** Do not use any characters outside the standard ASCII range. No trademark symbols, no copyright symbols, no special mathematical symbols, no emoji.
- **Process flows:** Use this pattern: "Step A -> Step B -> Step C" or "Step A, then Step B, then Step C."

**VALIDATION:** Before returning the JSON, scan every string value. If ANY character has a Unicode code point above 126, replace it with its ASCII equivalent.

## TONE AND STYLE RULES
- Write for speaking, not reading. Read it aloud — it should feel natural.
- Sentences: short. Direct. Max 12 words each.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout — address the viewer personally.
- Numbers beat adjectives. "62%" beats "most". "45 seconds" beats "fast".
- No buzzword stacking: avoid "cutting-edge AI-powered solution leveraging..."
- The company context is for background — do not narrate it like a brochure.
